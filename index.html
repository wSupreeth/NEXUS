WELCOME // NEXUS
A city loses its internet.
What happens next?
NEXUS is an interactive systems study. You'll design a mesh communication network, then watch it face a real crisis. No prior knowledge needed — the simulation teaches you as you go.

This takes about 10 minutes. Every decision you make has measurable consequences.
What you'll learn: Why decentralized networks are resilient. Why every design decision creates tradeoffs. Why constraint is a feature, not a limitation.
STEP 1 // THE GRID
The grid is your city.
Each cell represents a real area. People live there. They need to communicate during an emergency. You place nodes to build coverage.
Coverage zones light up green when you place a node. Dead zones stay dark red — those areas go silent during blackout.

Goal: reach 40%+ coverage before the crisis starts. You can't cover everything — that's the point.
STEP 2 // YOUR BUDGET
You have 12 budget units. Spend wisely.
Four node types. Each costs differently. Each covers differently. Each fails differently under stress.
📱 CIVILIAN DEVICE — Free. Short range. First to fail during blackout.
📡 MESH NODE — 1 unit. Medium range. Good bridge between relays.
🔋 RELAY HUB — 3 units. Long range. Backbone of the network.
🏢 SHELTER HUB — 2 units. Reliable anchor. Lasts longest.

There is no correct combination. Every topology creates different tradeoffs.
STEP 3 // THE CRISIS
Then the blackout hits.
Three phases. 24 simulated hours. Your topology faces everything: node failures, message floods, misinformation attacks, battery depletion.
Hour 0–3: Random failures. Coverage shrinks.
Hour 3–12: Medical, supply and misinfo messages flood in. You'll be asked to predict what happens next.
Hour 12+: Batteries drain. Will your relays survive day 2?

You control routing priority, verification strictness, and battery allocation in real time.
STEP 4 // READY
Choose your difficulty when you run the crisis.
Mild, Moderate, or Severe. This controls how fast nodes fail, message volume, and battery drain rate. Start mild if you want to learn. Go severe if you're ready to be stress-tested.
New in V4: Design Comparison — save two topologies and run both through the same crisis to see which held up better. Undo (Ctrl+Z) is available during design. Predict what happens before advancing each phase.

NODE TYPES - UNDERSTAND EACH BEFORE PLACING
📱
CIVILIAN DEVICE
Smartphones, laptops, tablets. Low range. Many devices = distributed network. Cost: 0 budget. Capacity: 2 msg/s. Range: 1.5 cells. Battery: 24h.
Best for: dense coverage in residential areas. First to fail during blackout.
📡
MESH NODE
Community-operated WiFi router + LoRa radio. Medium range. Cost: 1 budget. Capacity: 8 msg/s. Range: 2.5 cells. Battery: 48h.
Best for: neighborhood coordination hubs. Bridges gaps between relays.
🔋
RELAY HUB
Rooftop solar + battery + dual radio. Long range. Backbone infrastructure. Cost: 3 budget. Capacity: 25 msg/s. Range: 4.5 cells. Battery: 72h.
Best for: high-capacity backbone. Critical for city-scale coverage. Most expensive, most resilient.
🏢
SHELTER HUB
Hospital/Emergency shelter with backup power. Stationary. High capacity. Cost: 2 budget. Capacity: 15 msg/s. Range: 3.5 cells. Battery: 96h.
Best for: critical infrastructure nodes. Survive longest. Last places to go dark.
Design Strategy: The budget forces tradeoffs. Relays bring range + capacity but cost 3 units each. Nodes are flexible bridges. Devices cover dense areas cheaply but fail first. Shelters anchor the network. No topology is perfect - good design accepts some dead zones strategically.

ACT II // CRISIS SIMULATION

Blackout begins. Your decisions matter.

The internet is gone. Node failures cascade. Messages queue. Every routing decision trades one urgency against another. There is no perfect outcome - only better tradeoffs.

WHAT TO EXPECT - THE THREE PHASES OF CRISIS
Hour 0–3 // COLLAPSE
Power dies. Random devices and nodes go offline. Your mesh shrinks. Coverage gaps widen. First stress test: does your topology survive partial failure?
Hour 3–12 // MESSAGE BURST
Medical emergencies, supply requests, and misinformation flood the network at once. Bandwidth runs scarce. Priority and verification decisions ripple through the system.
Hour 12+ // SUSTAINABILITY
Batteries deplete. Solar recharges relays by day. Message volume stays high. Can your topology sustain through day 2? Battery boost decisions now show their cost.
YOUR CONTROL PANEL - WHAT EACH DECISION MEANS
🎯 ROUTING PRIORITY
Drag items to reorder. Top priority gets bandwidth first. Medical above supply means supply requests wait. Every second matters in emergencies - but fairness matters too.
✓ VERIFICATION STRICTNESS

SYSTEMS ANALYSIS // HOW IT WORKS

Design thinking behind decentralized resilience

What this simulation actually demonstrates about emergency networks - and why the design decisions were deliberate.

CONSTRAINT MODEL - THE TRADEOFF SPACE
COVERAGE vs COST
More relays expand coverage but exhaust budget. Fewer relays create dead zones that become lethal during emergencies. The optimal topology isn't maximum coverage - it's coverage of high-density population corridors with redundant paths.
SPEED vs TRUST
Low verification threshold propagates messages fast but allows misinformation to spread. High threshold catches bad actors but slows emergency alerts by 3–8 minutes per hop. This is a real tradeoff with no correct answer - context determines optimal threshold.
TODAY vs TOMORROW
Battery boosting increases today's message throughput but degrades tomorrow's relay capacity. Blackouts don't resolve in 12 hours. A topology designed to peak on day 1 may completely fail by day 3 when battery reserves are exhausted.
FAILURE MODE WALKTHROUGH
TOPOLOGY FAILURE: SINGLE POINT
If your topology has bridges - single nodes that connect two large clusters - losing that node partitions the network. Both halves can still communicate internally, but cross-partition messages fail silently. This is the most common design failure: it looks robust until the bridge dies.
SYSTEM FAILURE: CONGESTION COLLAPSE
During a crisis, message volume spikes 12×. Relays with limited bandwidth queue messages. If medical alerts queue behind lower-priority supply requests, response time degrades. The queue doesn't just slow things - it creates the illusion of a functioning network while critical requests silently expire.
TRUST FAILURE: MISINFORMATION CASCADE
A flood attack injects 47 identical packets into different mesh entry points. If verification threshold is too low, all 47 propagate before the duplicate detection triggers. By then, false evacuation routes have already reached 80% of nodes. Trust failures propagate faster than corrections.
RESOURCE FAILURE: BATTERY DEPLETION
Solar relay nodes maintain capacity in daylight. At night, they run on battery. If battery was depleted in hour 6 boosting throughput, the relay goes dark at hour 18. This creates a time-delayed topology change that the network hasn't planned for - and suddenly the bridge node is gone.
DESIGN STATEMENT

NEXUS is a systems design study - not a UI exercise. The simulation demonstrates that decentralized resilience is a constraint propagation problem: every design decision creates second-order effects that compound under stress. Redundancy costs budget. Speed costs trust. Throughput costs longevity. The goal isn't to optimize a single variable - it's to understand which constraints are acceptable to violate, and when.

This project explores emergency systems design, network topology reasoning, constraint-aware UX, and the mechanics of resilience emergence in adversarial conditions.
